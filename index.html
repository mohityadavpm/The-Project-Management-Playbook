The Project Management Playbook — Research Origin

It started with one poll.
It ended with 5,059 answers.

A single LinkedIn question about why projects stall turned into a 30-part series run over several months — and a pattern that held up across industries, geographies, and job titles.

"Why do projects still fail, even when everyone's using the same tools and the same frameworks?"

That question, posted as an ordinary poll, didn't resolve cleanly — the votes split, the comments disagreed, and a second question followed. Then a third. Within a few posts it was clear the disagreement itself was the finding: practitioners weren't confused about frameworks, they were describing gaps that no framework closes on its own.

Twenty-seven more polls followed — spanning initiation, planning, execution, monitoring, and closure — drawing Project Managers, PMO leads, consultants, and business sponsors from IT, infrastructure, consulting, manufacturing, sustainability, and government sectors, across India, the UK, the US, the Middle East, and Asia-Pacific.

Individual poll sizes ranged from 20 to 505 votes. Findings are self-reported and directional — a read on industry sentiment, not a statistically representative sample.

What the votes revealed

Everyone agreed on the crack.
Almost no one agreed on who should fix it.

The same split showed up in poll after poll: practitioners could name the problem precisely, but pointed to very different fixes depending on their seat in the org chart.

Where it breaks, phase by phase

The crack doesn't open at the end.
It opens at the start — and just gets wider.

Mapped against the five-phase lifecycle, the poll data shows failure compounding rather than appearing out of nowhere. Each phase inherits the last one's unfinished business.

Phase 1 — Initiation

Objectives left undefined, on purpose or not

79% objective-first41% want charters

Most practitioners know objective clarity matters most — but projects still launch on informal alignment rather than a documented, approved charter.

Illustrative pattern

A transformation programme kicks off with a deck and a Slack channel, no signed charter. Three months in, sponsors and delivery leads are still arguing over what "done" actually means.

Phase 2 — Planning

Structure exists, but is applied selectively

52% use MoSCoW58% skip risk-budget links

Prioritisation and risk techniques are well known. The gap isn't knowledge — it's rigor under time pressure.

Phase 3 — Execution

Capacity and commitment, not tasks, run out first

57% resource-limited52% low commitment

Plans hold up on paper. What breaks is workload balance and stakeholder buy-in — both portfolio-level, not task-level, problems.

Illustrative pattern

Two workstreams quietly share the same three engineers — nobody flagged the overlap during planning. It surfaces mid-sprint as missed deadlines, and leadership reads it as a performance problem instead of a resourcing one.

Phase 4 — Monitoring & Control

High visibility, low outcome focus

61% tool-reliant92% value version control

Dashboards and tracking tools are everywhere. They report activity completion — rarely whether the work is actually landing.

Phase 5 — Closure

The phase everyone skips on the way to the next project

34% cite disengagement35% cite no ownership

The most consistently neglected stage. Without formal handover and lessons captured, delivery success never converts into lasting value.

Illustrative pattern

The system goes live, the invoice is paid, and the team is already staffed on the next initiative. No retro gets run. Six months later, half the team has quietly reverted to the old spreadsheet.

Waterfall, Agile, or neither in practice

Frameworks argue in theory. In the field, they blend.

Predictive

Waterfall

  • 41% favor its charter discipline
  • 76% still borrow RACI from it
  • 69% use EVM, its native tool
  • Struggles when scope shifts mid-flight
Adaptive

Agile

  • 51% call it their improvement engine
  • 64% use it to engage resistant stakeholders
  • 35% still cite ownership gaps under it
  • Adaptable, but governance-light by default
Operational reality

Hybrid

  • Charters and RACI for accountability
  • Agile cycles for execution
  • EVM for cost and schedule control
  • Not a framework choice — a deliberate blend
Cross-cutting insight

Framework selection alone is a weak predictor of success. Projects don't fail because Agile or Waterfall was the wrong pick — they fail when objective clarity, ownership, stakeholder alignment, and closure are inconsistently executed, regardless of label.

What the data asks of each seat

Same findings. Three different jobs to do with them.

PMOs

Make the gate mandatory, not optional

  • Require a charter before initiation closes
  • Standardise RACI across every project, not just the large ones
  • Track adoption and value, not only time and cost
Project Managers

Lead the room, not just the roadmap

  • Anchor every plan in a clearly stated objective
  • Engage resistant stakeholders early, not after pushback
  • Treat tools as decision support, not a substitute for judgment
Business Leaders

Sponsor the outcome, not the deadline

  • Define success beyond on-time, on-budget
  • Fund change management as real work, not a soft extra
  • Assign explicit ownership for what happens after go-live
Projects rarely fail from a missing framework.
They fail from a framework that nobody finished applying.
